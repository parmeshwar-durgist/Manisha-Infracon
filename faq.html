Common Questions Regarding Ready Mix Concrete

Ready Mix Concrete (RMC) is concrete that is batched at a centralized computer-controlled plant according to precise recipe recipes, rather than being hand-mixed on site. It guarantees chemical consistency, uniform aggregate grading, and eliminates material dust waste on the construction site.

RMC guarantees: (1) Superior quality control due to automated electronic scales, (2) Drastically reduced labor costs and cast schedules, (3) Eliminates site storage space for sand and aggregates, and (4) Delivers custom designs (like fiber-reinforced, green, or self-compacting concrete) that are impossible to mix by hand.

Normally, concrete should be discharged and placed within 90 to 120 minutes from the time it was batched at the plant (not from when it arrived at your site). For distant sites or hot weather, we add specialized hydration retarder admixtures to extend this placement window to 3 hours.

Curing is the maintenance of moisture and temperature conditions in freshly poured concrete to allow hydration. Proper curing (sprinkling water, ponding, or applying curing compounds) is essential to secure target compressive strengths, prevent shrinkage cracks, and maximize durability.

Concrete reaches about 16% of its strength in 24 hours, 65% in 7 days, and its nominal design strength (100%) at 28 days. Curing must be actively maintained for at least the first 7 to 10 days for standard mixes.

The 'M' stands for 'Mix' and the number represents the characteristic compressive strength in Megapascals (MPa) or N/mm² at 28 days of curing. For example, M25 concrete will withstand a crushing pressure of 25 Megapascals. M20-M25 are standard for house slabs, while M40+ are structural columns.

A slump test measures the consistency and workability of fresh concrete before it is cast. Fresh concrete is compacted into a hollow cone, which is then lifted. The height drop (slump) is measured. It indicates if the water-cement ratio is correct and if the mix is pumpable.

Self-Compacting Concrete (SCC) is a highly flowable concrete mix that spreads into formwork and passes congested steel rebar under its own gravity weight, without segregation. You should use it for dense reinforcement setups, thin structural columns, or where mechanical vibrator access is blocked.

Light rain is generally manageable if the slab is covered with plastic sheeting immediately after leveling. However, heavy downpours will wash away the surface cement paste, increase the water-binder ratio, and permanently weaken the concrete surface. We recommend rescheduling during heavy rains.

Green Concrete is an eco-friendly mix design that replaces a portion of high-emissions Portland cement with recycled materials like fly ash (from power plants) or slag (from steel manufacturing). It reduces carbon footprints by up to 50%, reduces heat generation, and helps earn LEED/IGBC rating points.

Concrete volume is calculated in cubic meters (m³). The formula is: Length (m) × Width (m) × Thickness/Depth (m) = Volume (m³). We recommend adding a 5% to 10% wastage buffer to account for spillage, formwork deflection, and minor grade changes.

Our standard transit mixer truck holds 6 to 10 cubic meters. Our minimum ordering capacity is 3 cubic meters. Orders smaller than this incur a small dry-run transportation surcharge, or can be supplied as Bag Concrete.

A loaded transit mixer weighs up to 32 tons. Access roads must be at least 3.5 meters wide, clear of low-hanging electric wires, and solid enough to prevent sinking. If a boom pump is needed, additional clearance is required to extend stabilizer outriggers.

Fiber-reinforced concrete contains distributed synthetic or steel fibers. While micro-synthetic fibers (PP) prevent shrinkage micro-cracking, they do NOT replace structural steel rebar. However, macro-synthetic or steel fibers can replace structural mesh in industrial floors and pavements.

For slabs, ponding is the most effective curing method: build small sand clay dikes around slab sections and fill them with 25-50mm of water. Curing should begin as soon as the concrete surface has set enough to resist damage, and must continue for a minimum of 7 days (or 10 days for blended cements).
